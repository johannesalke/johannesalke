**Book Recommendations**

- Endless Forms Most Beautiful: A book on developmental biology, i.e. the logic and methods by which living beings grow from a single cell to their full adult form.  And while the mechanisms of development in themselves are already interesting, the truly compelling part is what you can learn from them about evolution. Most evolutionary changes come down to the tuning of one of the pre-existing mechanisms, rather than the introduction of something entirely new. Adaptions that align with easily variable mechanisms are easier to make.
- Into the Cool: Imbues the reader with a profound understanding of the role energy flows and entropy play in organizing the physical world and biological systems. To give some minor examples, the energy flow from warm equatorial regions to the cold poles drives ocean currents and weather (including hurricanes). Ecosystems are organized around plants, who are the only ones capable of garnishing their own energy from the sun, with animals having to get their energy from either eating plants or from eating animals that eat plants. Reading this book gave me a deeper understanding of thermodynamics and ecology, and one of the latter chapter sowed the seeds for my current thinking on the role that monetary and resource flows play in organizing the economy.
- Wetware: Nerves are good and well for communication in multicellular organisms, but how do singular cells (alone or as part of an animal) process information? This book covers various mechanisms and phenomena in service of answering that question.
- The Epigenetics Revolution: Covers topics of genetics and epigenetics, which relate to the prior books in the roles they play in processing and responding to information. A bit dense at times, but does a decent job at covering in depth an area that the previous ones touched on but didn't cover in depth.
- The Vital Question: Seeks to answer questions relating to the origin of life and some peculiar features of evolution. Interesting for its ideas on the origin of life, energetics and metabolism, and what constrains evolution. 
- Fearful Symmetry: This one isn't a particularly strong recommendation, but I can still credit some chapters in this book with shaping my thinking about math, physics and symmetry.
- The Economy of Cities: This author has some fascinating ideas on how cities drive economic growth, the historical role of cities, and what promotes or harms a cities economic health. I couldn't say whether all of it is entirely correct, but I certainly believe she has a point with many of her arguments.
